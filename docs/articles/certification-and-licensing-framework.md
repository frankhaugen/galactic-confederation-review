## Abstract

Canonical reference for personnel competence, shipboard qualification, certificate portability, professional licensing, and emergency authority within the Galactic Confederation.

---

</div>

## Article

### 1. Governing principles

The Galactic Confederation regulates competence by documenting what a person can do, making that competence portable, and defining when professional authority creates a duty to act.

The system follows five principles:

1. Learning is open.
2. Competence must be demonstrated.
3. Shipboard experience is valid evidence.
4. Certificates are portable public records.
5. Safety overrides ordinary scope and staffing rules during a real emergency.

The system is deliberately low-entry and extensive. It does not require a person to buy access to a profession before beginning to learn it. Most spacers develop through continual apprenticeship, familiarization, supervised work, and periodic external verification.

Formal courses remain useful where structured training is more efficient, especially for pilots, military personnel, large corporations, and technical colleges.

---

### 2. Core definitions

#### 2.1 Competence

Competence is the demonstrated ability to perform a defined task or exercise a defined body of knowledge.

Competence may be established through:

- examinations
- formal courses
- simulator work
- practical assessments
- logged experience
- supervised work
- recorded emergency performance
- captain or supervisor attestations
- authenticated video
- ship telemetry
- combinations of the above

Competence exists before the GC records it. The registry makes it visible and portable.

#### 2.2 Familiarized

A person is Familiarized when they understand the work, hazards, tools, terminology, normal procedures, and stop conditions well enough to assist safely and usefully.

A Familiarized person is not authorized to perform the work independently.

Example:

```text
Power Core Maintenance
Grade: Familiarized
```

This may mean the holder can:

- enter the relevant engineering space safely
- identify major components
- maintain tool and parts control
- follow isolation boundaries
- read procedures
- record measurements
- assist with access and component handling
- recognize common alarms and stop conditions

It does not mean the holder can diagnose faults, select repairs, bypass interlocks, calibrate the system, or return it to service alone.

#### 2.3 Qualified

A person is Qualified when they can perform the defined work independently within the scope of the certificate.

Example:

```text
Power Core Maintenance
Grade: Qualified
```

A Qualified person may perform approved procedures, routine maintenance, standard tests, component replacement, documentation, and return-to-service actions within the certificate scope.

Qualification does not necessarily include unusual faults, major redesign, inspection authority, or system-wide professional responsibility.

#### 2.4 Certificate

A certificate is a publicly registered statement of demonstrated competence.

A certificate records:

- competency
- grade
- scope
- restrictions
- evidence
- certifier
- date
- currency status where relevant

Examples:

```text
Environmental Systems Maintenance
Grade: Familiarized
```

```text
Vacuum Welding
Grade: Qualified
```

Certificates belong to the individual. Once accepted into the GC registry, they remain portable between ships, employers, stations, and jurisdictions.

#### 2.5 Provisional certificate status

A captain or appropriately qualified supervisor may attest that a crew member has demonstrated competence aboard a ship.

This creates provisional recognition.

A provisional status may be:

- Familiarized
- Qualified

It is normally valid:

- aboard the signing vessel
- under the recorded supervision conditions
- within the stated equipment scope
- until the audit deadline

It is not automatically portable across the GC.

A licensed certifier may later audit the evidence and convert the provisional status into a permanent GC certificate.

#### 2.6 License

A license is a publicly registered grant of integrated professional authority.

A license is built from a stack of certificates, experience, current competence, professional examination, and liability standing.

Examples:

- Pilot License
- Navigator License
- Licensed Ship Engineer
- Licensed Ship Physician
- Licensed Station Engineer

A license grants authority and imposes a professional duty to act.

A certificate proves competence.

A license creates responsibility.

#### 2.7 Endorsement

An endorsement expands or limits a certificate or license to a specific operating scope.

Examples:

- passenger transport
- hazardous cargo
- hospital vessel
- heavy transport
- military auxiliary
- atmospheric flight
- FTL drive class
- vessel mass class
- specific equipment family

#### 2.8 Local qualification

A local qualification records that a person is approved to use a particular vessel, machine, procedure, or workplace.

Examples:

- qualified on Calypso
- qualified on a specific power core model
- cleared for a station repair bay
- approved for a manufacturer's diagnostic system

A local qualification does not replace a portable certificate.

### 3. Spacer foundation

#### 3.1 Root rule

The Spacer Foundation is the root of every shipboard and station-operational certificate branch.

```text
No Spacer Foundation
    -> no shipboard technical familiarization
    -> no shipboard technical qualification
    -> no shipboard professional license
```

This rule does not prevent a planetary worker from holding a terrestrial trade certificate.

A planetary welder may be certified in welding without being a spacer.

They cannot obtain shipboard welding, zero-gravity welding, vacuum welding, hull repair, or other operational space certificates until the required Spacer Foundation is complete.

#### 3.2 Cost

Basic spacer certificates are free.

This includes:

- learning materials
- standard examinations
- practical assessments
- registry issuance
- foundation renewals
- ordinary remedial assessment
- conversion of school records

The GC treats spacer safety competence as public infrastructure.

Optional premium services may be charged for, including private tutoring, accelerated scheduling, accommodation, or specialist simulator access.

The certificate itself cannot be paywalled.

---

### 4. Standard Spacer

#### 4.1 Educational role

Standard Spacer is normally incorporated into schooling in space-based communities.

By the end of tenth grade, a student raised on a station, habitat, ship community, or other space settlement is expected to hold the Standard Spacer bundle.

#### 4.2 Scope

Standard Spacer establishes that the holder can safely live, travel, study, and perform ordinary supervised work aboard ships and stations.

Typical competencies include:

- alarm recognition
- muster and evacuation
- pressure door discipline
- emergency breathing equipment
- basic fire response
- decompression response
- contamination awareness
- basic first aid
- emergency communications
- basic tool safety
- electrical and machinery hazard awareness
- basic zero-gravity movement
- variable-gravity movement
- cargo and passageway safety
- emergency command discipline

A Standard Spacer is not yet a professional spacer.

They are safe enough not to require constant supervision.

---

### 5. Advanced Spacer

#### 5.1 Educational role

Advanced Spacer is normally completed, or nearly completed, by the end of vocational school or college-preparatory secondary education.

Vocational graduates are normally fully qualified.

University-bound students may have completed the theory and most practical components, with only limited workplace or EVA requirements remaining.

#### 5.2 Scope

Advanced Spacer is the professional foundation for specialist branches.

Typical competencies include:

- independent shipboard safety
- zero-gravity work
- pressure-suit operation
- equipment isolation and lockout
- damage-control participation
- maintenance documentation
- technical logkeeping
- tool and parts control
- supervised watchstanding
- engineering-space conduct
- practical emergency response
- fault reporting
- systems awareness
- emergency medical response

Full EVA Operations may be part of Advanced Spacer for vocational and operational tracks, or a closely adjacent free branch for academic tracks.

Advanced Spacer does not make the holder an engineer, pilot, navigator, medic, or hull technician.

It makes them eligible to begin the relevant certificate branches.

### 6. Certificate graph

The certificate system is commonly called a tree, but technically behaves as a dependency graph.

A certificate may require:

- one prior certificate
- several prior certificates
- a minimum grade in each prerequisite
- logged hours
- repeated task exposure
- practical sign-offs
- examination
- current operational experience

One prerequisite may feed several branches.

Several branches may merge into one advanced certificate.

#### 6.1 Example: hull repair

```text
Standard Spacer
    |
Advanced Spacer
    |
EVA Operations: Qualified
    |
+-- Zero-G Work: Qualified
|
+-- Vacuum Work: Qualified

General Welding: Qualified
    |
Structural Welding: Qualified
    |
+-- Zero-G Welding: Familiarized
|       |
|       +-- Zero-G Welding: Qualified
|
+-- Vacuum Welding: Familiarized
        |
        +-- Vacuum Welding: Qualified

Pressure Boundary Systems: Qualified
Structural Damage Assessment: Qualified
Non-Destructive Testing: Qualified
Damage Control: Qualified
Zero-G Welding: Qualified
Vacuum Welding: Qualified
EVA Operations: Qualified
    |
Hull Repair: Familiarized
    |
Hull Repair: Qualified
```

Zero-gravity welding and vacuum welding may be trained in parallel once the shared prerequisites are complete.

A person must first demonstrate ordinary welding competence before escalating into zero-gravity or vacuum welding.

#### 6.2 Familiarization is still prerequisite-dependent

Familiarization is not always entry-level.

Power Core Maintenance Familiarization may require prior qualifications in:

- engineering-space safety
- tool control
- power distribution
- high-energy isolation

Vacuum Welding Familiarization may require:

- General Welding: Qualified
- Structural Welding: Qualified
- EVA Operations: Qualified
- Vacuum Work: Qualified

Passing wrenches around a power core may be beginner work within an advanced branch, but it is not beginner work in absolute terms.

---

### 7. Continual apprenticeship

Most independent spacers do not progress through a fixed classroom sequence.

They develop through continual shipboard apprenticeship.

Typical progression:

```text
observe
    -> assist
    -> become Familiarized
    -> perform under supervision
    -> become provisionally Qualified
    -> accumulate records
    -> complete controlled examination
    -> receive portable GC certificate
```

A crew member may accumulate substantial competence without realizing it.

A certifier reviewing years of shipboard records may find that the person needs only a small number of theory examinations or practical demonstrations to receive several permanent certificates.

A common spacer rule is:

```text
Get everything signed.
```

Good ships maintain extensive training records.

Bad ships use junior labor without documenting career development.

This affects recruitment, retention, pay expectations, and reputation.

---

### 8. Shipboard training records

Every operating ship maintains personnel training records alongside maintenance records.

Records may include:

- tasks observed
- tasks assisted
- tasks performed under direct supervision
- tasks performed independently
- systems and models encountered
- maintenance evolutions
- drills
- real casualty response
- hours in relevant environments
- video
- telemetry
- work orders
- diagnostic traces
- tool and parts records
- supervisor attestations
- captain attestations
- provisional grades
- failed assessments
- remedial work
- currency dates

The records distinguish between:

- being present
- assisting
- performing
- supervising
- approving

A person who passed tools during an overhaul does not become qualified to overhaul the system.

They may still accumulate evidence toward:

- engineering-space safety
- tool control
- contamination procedure
- lockout procedure
- component handling
- maintenance documentation
- system Familiarization

### 9. Captain and supervisor attestations

#### 9.1 Captain authority

A captain may attest that a person demonstrated competence aboard the vessel.

The captain is attesting to the authenticity of the shipboard record and the person's observed performance.

A captain does not create permanent GC portability by fiat.

#### 9.2 Technical supervisor authority

Where the work is specialist work, an appropriately qualified technical supervisor attests to technical performance.

Example:

```text
Technical supervisor:
Mira

Attestation:
Crew member performed the listed environmental maintenance tasks safely and to Familiarized grade.
```

```text
Captain:
James Simmons

Attestation:
The attached ship records, video, telemetry, and work orders are authentic.
```

#### 9.3 Liability

Falsifying an attestation exposes:

- the signer
- the ship
- the operator
- the employer
- the insurer
- the certifying organization

The value of the sign-off comes from the liability attached to it.

---

### 10. Portability and external certification

The GC controls certificate portability through its registry and recognized certifiers.

The most common certification mechanism is remote controlled examination aboard the candidate's ship.

Typical process:

```text
1. Candidate requests certification.
2. Ship submits training and maintenance records.
3. Certifier checks prerequisites.
4. Certifier reviews video, telemetry, and attestations.
5. Candidate completes theory examination.
6. Candidate performs a controlled practical task.
7. Certifier questions the candidate live.
8. Certifier records scope, grade, and restrictions.
9. Certificate enters the GC registry.
```

The practical task may involve:

- planned maintenance
- a simulator
- a seeded noncritical fault
- a controlled shutdown
- a standard diagnosis
- documentation and restoration

The certifier tests understanding, not merely imitation.

The candidate pays for verification, not permission to learn.

Routine technical audits commonly cost a few hundred credits.

---

### 11. Courses

Courses exist and may be the fastest route through a branch.

They are most common in:

- corporations
- technical colleges
- militaries
- manufacturer programs
- large station authorities
- pilot schools

Courses are efficient where rare or dangerous scenarios must be presented deliberately.

They may provide:

- theory
- laboratory work
- simulator time
- controlled practical exercises
- equipment variety
- standardized assessment
- provisional task credits

Courses do not necessarily replace real operating experience.

The standards are shared even when the route differs.

#### 11.1 Institutional route

```text
course
    -> laboratory
    -> simulator
    -> controlled practical
    -> supervised placement
    -> portable certificate
```

#### 11.2 Spacer route

```text
useful work
    -> logged experience
    -> Familiarization
    -> supervised performance
    -> provisional Qualification
    -> targeted examination
    -> portable certificate
```

### 12. Flight control and Pilot licensing

#### 12.1 Operating controls is not licensed piloting

Piloting is not a prohibited physical action.

Many people need to operate thrust controls without being licensed pilots.

Examples:

- maintenance workers moving ships in repair bays
- engineers conducting propulsion tests
- dock crews aligning ships with loading systems
- salvage crews stabilizing vessels
- trainees receiving supervised control time
- crew taking emergency control

The Pilot License authorizes independent flight responsibility.

It does not create exclusive legal ownership of the controls.

#### 12.2 Flight-control progression

```text
Advanced Spacer
    |
Ship Movement: Familiarized
    |
Restricted Ship Movement: Qualified
    |
Flight Controls: Familiarized
    |
Pilot training and certificate stack
    |
Pilot License
```

#### 12.3 Restricted Ship Movement

A maintenance worker may be qualified to:

- reposition a ship within a repair bay
- follow a station-controlled movement path
- move between adjacent berths
- test thrusters at low power
- manually stop unintended movement
- hold attitude
- follow direct station-control instructions

Restrictions may include:

- no independent departure
- no open traffic
- no orbital maneuver
- no atmospheric flight
- no FTL
- no passenger movement
- no unsupervised docking
- no override beyond emergency stabilization

A maintenance worker can move the ship when told where to put it.

A pilot decides whether moving it is safe.

#### 12.4 Why pilot courses are efficient

Sitting beside a pilot provides limited exposure.

Routine automation does not reliably teach:

- asymmetric thrust
- failed docking systems
- sensor loss
- unstable rotation
- traffic conflicts
- damaged controls
- emergency approach
- aborted departure
- high-mass handling

Simulator training compresses rare and dangerous scenarios safely.

A Pilot License normally requires:

- structured theory
- extensive simulator work
- controlled practical flight
- supervised real operations
- traffic-law examination
- emergency scenarios
- vessel-class qualification
- final controlled examination

An apprenticeship route remains possible but is usually much slower.

### 13. License construction

A license is a stack of certificates.

The stack may contain:

- absolute requirements
- mandatory breadth
- required depth
- exchangeable requirements
- compensating qualifications
- experience requirements
- professional examination
- currency requirements
- endorsements
- liability standing

A license is not based on one fixed course.

#### 13.1 Absolute requirements

Some requirements cannot be exchanged away.

For a Licensed Ship Engineer, likely examples include:

- Advanced Spacer
- Engineering Safety: Qualified
- Isolation and Lockout: Qualified
- Maintenance Documentation: Qualified
- Damage Control: Qualified
- Systems Diagnostics: Qualified
- Engineering Watchstanding: Qualified
- Casualty Coordination: Qualified
- Cross-System Fault Assessment: Qualified

#### 13.2 Mandatory breadth

A Licensed Ship Engineer must be Familiarized with every operationally relevant system and maintenance routine within the licensed vessel scope.

This includes systems they are not independently qualified to maintain.

Examples:

- power generation
- power distribution
- emergency power
- water reclamation
- waste processing
- environmental control
- pressure management
- thermal management
- propulsion
- FTL support
- fuel handling
- control systems
- automation
- fire suppression
- cargo machinery
- docking systems
- communications hardware
- sensor hardware
- fabrication systems
- hull maintenance
- medical-system support
- passenger systems

For water reclamation, Familiarization may be enough.

The candidate must still have observed and assisted with actual maintenance.

A Licensed Ship Engineer cannot plausibly say:

```text
I have never seen the water reclamation system opened.
```

#### 13.3 Required depth

The candidate must be Qualified in a defined set of major engineering domains.

These normally include minimum depth across:

- power
- environment and pressure
- propulsion or FTL
- control and automation
- casualty or structural systems

The engineer need not be the best specialist in every branch.

They must understand the ship as one interacting machine.

#### 13.4 Exchangeable requirements

Some certificate requirements may compensate for others.

Example:

```text
Route A:
Environmental Systems Maintenance: Qualified
Hull Repair: Familiarized
EVA Operations: Familiarized
```

```text
Route B:
Environmental Systems Maintenance: Familiarized
Hull Repair: Qualified
EVA Operations: Qualified
Vacuum Work: Qualified
```

Route B may satisfy the same license breadth through stronger external and structural competence.

Compensation is bounded by domain.

Hull competence cannot replace all environmental knowledge.

Pilot competence cannot replace power-system competence.

Every required domain must remain represented.

#### 13.5 Vessel and role endorsements

A license may carry endorsements such as:

- small commercial vessel
- passenger transport
- hospital vessel
- heavy cargo
- hazardous cargo
- FTL class
- military auxiliary
- atmospheric operation

The certificate stack determines which endorsements are available.

---

### 14. Licensed Ship Engineer

A Licensed Ship Engineer combines:

```text
broad Familiarization
+ deep Qualification
+ cross-system integration
+ professional authority
+ duty to act
```

The license holder must understand:

- system purpose
- maintenance routine
- hazards
- interfaces
- common failures
- shutdown and restart
- escalation points
- specialist requirements
- legal operating limits

They may not personally repair every component.

They must know when a specialist is required and how the specialist's work affects the rest of the ship.

The license exists because ships fail through interactions, not isolated diagrams.

---

### 15. Licensed Station Engineer

A station contains many ship-scale and city-scale systems.

An unrestricted Licensed Station Engineer may require decades of accumulated certificates across:

- structural systems
- rotating structures
- distributed power
- atmosphere districts
- water
- sewage
- industrial processes
- transit
- docking
- traffic control
- hospitals
- agriculture
- municipal services
- fire and rescue systems
- multi-species environments
- legacy infrastructure
- continuous construction

Most station engineers hold narrower professional licenses.

Examples:

- Station Power Engineer
- Habitat Environmental Engineer
- Docking Systems Engineer
- Rotational Structure Engineer
- Pressure Infrastructure Engineer
- Station Control Engineer

A fully integrated Station Engineer is rare enough to be treated as a near-mythic professional.

Ship engineering is a career.

Station engineering may consume a lifetime before unrestricted integration training begins.

### 16. Currency, renewal, and lapse

Certificates normally remain permanently visible in the registry.

Operational authority may require current practice.

Example:

```text
Vacuum Welding
Grade: Qualified
Currency: Lapsed
```

The person still holds the certificate.

They may need supervised work, refresher examination, or practical reassessment before independent use.

Foundation certificates normally do not expire, though emergency procedures may require updates.

Licenses require active renewal.

A license holder may need to show:

- continuing professional work
- current mandatory certificates
- current emergency drills
- maintained breadth
- updated endorsements
- no unresolved professional findings
- liability standing

### 17. Safety supremacy

#### 17.1 Highest-order rule

```text
Safety overrides all ordinary priorities.
```

Immediate protection of life and prevention of catastrophic harm override normal certificate, license, role, and scope restrictions.

Certificates govern normal work.

Licenses govern professional authority and duty.

Safety governs emergencies.

#### 17.2 Emergency action outside scope

A person may act outside their normal certificate or license scope when:

- there is an immediate threat to life or catastrophic harm
- no more qualified person can act in time
- the action has a reasonable chance of improving the situation
- the action does not create clearly greater unnecessary risk
- the person acts no further outside scope than required

Example:

A crew member has not completed EVA Familiarization.

An EVA casualty occurs.

If the rescue can be performed without adding unreasonable risk and no qualified rescuer can act in time, the incomplete certificate is irrelevant.

The person may act.

Later review considers reasonableness, necessity, proportionality, and actual competence.

It does not punish the absence of a certificate by itself.

#### 17.3 Failure is not automatic misconduct

Emergency protection does not depend on success.

A reasonable rescue may fail.

Review is based on what was known and reasonably believed at the time.

#### 17.4 Emergency action cannot be planned staffing

A ship may not deliberately operate below required staffing and rely on emergency authority as a substitute.

Invalid:

```text
We do not need a second pilot.
The engineer can take over if something happens.
```

Valid:

```text
The pilot is incapacitated.
The engineer has Flight Controls Familiarization.
Take the helm and hold attitude.
```

Emergency authority responds to a broken plan.

It cannot be the plan.

### 18. Duty to act

#### 18.1 License creates duty

Only licenses create a standing professional duty to act.

Certificates prove competence but do not impose full professional obligation.

```text
Certificate:
May act within competence.

License:
Must respond within professional scope.
```

#### 18.2 Licensed professional duties

A license normally imposes:

1. Duty to assess.
2. Duty to act when capable.
3. Duty to escalate when more capable help is needed.
4. Duty to remain until relieved, incapacitated, or the emergency ends.

#### 18.3 Medical example

A Licensed Ship Physician cannot refuse an emergency appendectomy solely because they are off watch.

They must assess and provide the reasonable professional response.

They may:

- operate
- stabilize
- use an AutoDoc
- delegate
- evacuate
- delay under monitoring
- determine that another treatment is safer

Their duty is to act professionally.

It is not a duty to guarantee survival.

The cook's assistant has no duty to perform the procedure.

They may assist voluntarily or under ordinary emergency duties, but they are not compelled to exercise professional medical judgment.

#### 18.4 Engineering example

A Licensed Ship Engineer cannot say "off watch" while the power core is destabilizing.

They must assess, direct, isolate, repair, evacuate, or advise abandonment as the situation requires.

A technician with Power Core Maintenance Qualification may assist.

The technician does not automatically carry the engineer's duty to assume professional command.

#### 18.5 Actual incapacity

Being off duty is not a defense.

Actual incapacity may be.

A license holder may state that they are:

- medically impaired
- exhausted beyond safe performance
- affected by medication
- outside actual competence
- less suitable than another available professional

That statement is itself subject to later review.

---

### 19. Captain's safety authority

#### 19.1 Command authority

The captain controls ship priorities.

Licensed professionals control technical judgment within their domains during normal operation.

During an emergency, the captain may override professional concerns when necessary for safety.

#### 19.2 Examples

The captain may:

- take the helm
- delegate the helm to an unlicensed person
- order work outside normal certificate scope
- bypass procedure
- sacrifice equipment or cargo
- order a dangerous maneuver
- require an emergency repair
- direct evacuation
- shut down systems against specialist preference

Example:

```text
Engineer:
The drive may be destroyed if we jump now.

Captain:
If we remain, the ship will be destroyed. Execute the jump.
```

The engineer has fulfilled the professional duty by stating the risk.

The captain owns the command decision.

#### 19.3 Taking or delegating the helm

Taking the helm is not prohibited by licensing rules.

Delegating the helm is not prohibited when circumstances require it.

The helm is not physically or legally reserved to licensed pilots.

A license determines routine authority, duty, professional responsibility, and planned staffing.

It does not prevent emergency control by another person.

#### 19.4 Limits

The captain's override must be defensible.

Review asks:

- Was there a real emergency?
- Was the override directed at safety?
- Was a safer qualified option available in time?
- Was the action proportionate?
- Was the override limited to the emergency?
- Was the captain solving an emergency or avoiding staffing rules?

The captain cannot use "safety" as a routine excuse to ignore professional standards.

---

### 20. Priority hierarchy

```text
1. Protect life.
2. Prevent immediate catastrophic escalation.
3. Stabilize the ship or installation.
4. Preserve rescue capability.
5. Follow emergency command.
6. Observe professional scope and certificate boundaries.
7. Protect property, schedules, and commercial interests.
```

Normal certificate and license limits begin below immediate safety priorities.

---

### 21. Compact doctrine

```text
Competence is learned openly.

Experience is documented aboard ship.

Captains and supervisors attest to demonstrated work.

Certifiers audit the evidence.

The GC makes certificates portable.

Licenses are stacks of certificates.

Licenses grant authority and impose duty.

The captain controls ship priorities.

Safety overrides all ordinary boundaries.

Emergency authority cannot be planned as staffing.
```
