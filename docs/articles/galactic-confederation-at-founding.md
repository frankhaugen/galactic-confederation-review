## Abstract

This survey traces the founding of the Galactic Confederation as a sequence of events: Compact accession into the Earth Union, the IUAS moral-union experiment, its collapse under route geometry, the procedural reassembly of credits and insurance into a signable Charter, the Guardianship bargain that made abolitionist and slave-based polities cohabit one registry, and the 2295 proclamation that named what had already become operational fact.

</div>

## Article

### 2150–2199: The Compact and its afterlife

In **2150** the **Compact of Five** bound Sol and four nearby non-human systems — Greth, Vaelor, Orunai, and Kesh'lin — into a trade and transit compact without political unification. The arrangement was proposed by stronger neighbors, not Earth. Humans contributed independent traders and procedural habits; Greth contributed administrative continuity; Vaelor priced risk; Orunai standardized engineering; Kesh'lin mediated disputes.

For fifty years the Compact governed docking, arbitration, tariffs, and entity recognition while leaving domestic law untouched. Trade volume rose. Standards converged upward. By the **2180s** the five members behaved less like treaty partners and more like one operating environment.

In **2189** the Earth Union reformed from nation-state membership to habitat-based membership. In **2199** the Compact dissolved when the four non-Sol members **acceded into the Earth Union** as member systems. The old Sol-centered name remained. The first major post-accession legislative motion failed — a warning that expanded polities could not govern by enthusiasm alone.

### 2200–2280: The IUAS and the framework stack

In **2200** negotiators launched the **Interstellar Union of Aligned Societies (IUAS)** as a successor project: a values-aligned interstellar union built on the Compact's success. Members were expected to converge on personhood norms, due-process language, bounded labor coercion or abolitionist trajectories, and humanitarian duties treated as real obligations rather than local custom.

While the IUAS debated ethics, the older procedural stack kept thickening without a founding declaration:

* Earth circulated a non-binding discussion document outsiders hated and used anyway
* **Effort credits** emerged as a reference unit — ten credits equal to one normative day of baseline labor — then persisted between settlements until clearing became routine
* Insurance crises after shell entities and unsatisfied arbitration produced mandatory transit and cargo minima
* Neutral inspectorate traditions — later associated with the Nosies line — gained authority to **invalidate** eligibility without police powers

The IUAS and the framework therefore grew in parallel: one asking whether systems could belong together ethically, the other asking whether they could clear a contract regardless.

### 2288–2293: Collapse

By **2288** the IUAS faced structural strain its founders had underestimated. Ethics did not sort cleanly by geometry. Aligned systems clustered. Incompatible neighbors remained inside critical corridors and surrounding arcs. Exclusion read as civilizational judgment. Pressure on outsiders read as moral empire even when framed as principle.

Surrounded incompatible polities created an unsolvable trilemma: leave them outside and destabilize the map; pressure them and become coercive; admit them without convergence and abandon the union's premise.

Between **2288** and **2293** accession slowed, corridor disputes multiplied, and several aligned blocs began treating non-member systems as reform targets rather than neighbors. The IUAS did not end in a single battle. It ended when continuing as a moral union became more expensive than admitting the procedural stack had already won.

### 2293–2295: The founding congress

What followed was not a constitutional convention in the heroic sense. Historians call it the **founding congress** because something had to be signed, but the work was mostly inventory and compromise.

Delegates arrived from polities that already cleared credits, bought insurance, accepted arbitration, and registered hulls. The question was no longer whether a civilization-spanning interface existed. The question was whether anyone would **name** it and whether the name could survive contact with incompatible domestic law.

Three fights dominated the record:

**1. Membership tests.** Abolitionist delegations — led in public memory by Earth Union negotiators — sought ethical floors as entry conditions. Extraction economies refused. The settled text removed universal ethics as a membership requirement and substituted **interface compliance**: legible liability, insurable ports, attributable persons and assets on forms the mesh could read.

**2. The credit rail.** Negotiators ratified what merchants had already built: normalized effort as the Confederation accounting unit. The Charter did not invent credits. It **adopted** them because polities that could not agree on personhood could still agree on what a day of work looked like on a ledger.

**3. Dependent-status labor.** This was the fight that nearly broke the congress. Roughly four-fifths of productive labor in the wider economy originated in systems that depended on coerced or dependent-status labor. An outright ban would not have produced abolition at the table. It would have produced splintered lanes and regional wars over whose moral grammar controlled the corridors.

Earth arrived with abolitionist credentials and without abolitionist leverage. The compromise reframed dependent-status labor as an **insurance and registry problem**: unregulated coercion externalized risk, distorted prices, and broke insurability. The interface-stable name was **Guardianship** — registered control with auditable limits, duties of care, oversight boards, and prohibitions on mutilation, casual killing, and unregulated transfer.

Slave-based economies signed because the institution remained enforceable. Abolitionist polities signed because the alternative was no common restraint at all. Neither side called the result justice. Both sides called it signable.

### 2295: Proclamation

In **2295** the assembled delegations issued the proclamation that named the **Galactic Confederation**: a confederation of star systems and system groups bound by interface law, not shared internal morality.

No capital planet won the founding. No member transferred domestic sovereignty to a central legislature. No confederal legion was chartered for member interiors. War was excluded from insurable risk by design — not from pacifism, but because war destroyed the accumulated effort the ledger existed to preserve.

Greth continuity archives sometimes call 2295 a **recognition year** rather than an establishment year. The distinction matters to archivists. For most founding historians it is pedantry. By proclamation day the mesh already behaved like a confederation. The document caught up to practice.

### 2295–2310: First expansion

The decade after proclamation is often misread as triumphal accession. It was closer to **checklist onboarding**. Systems that already traded in credits, accepted insurance audits, honored arbitration, and permitted port inspection were treated as members in fact before they were members in ceremony.

Expansion therefore looked like economic gravity: the cost of staying outside insured corridors rose until "member" became a description of operational reality. Domestic law remained domestic. The founding achievement was not moral unity. It was **continuity under difference** — the corridors stayed open across disagreement because the Charter had stopped asking polities to agree on virtue before agreeing on forms.

### What founding settled — and what it did not

The founding congress settled the sequence that still organizes argument two centuries later:

* the IUAS was dead as a moral union
* the procedural stack was alive as the Confederation
* effort credits would not ask how work was produced
* Guardianship would let incompatible labor regimes share one registry

It did **not** settle abolition. It did not settle personhood. It did not settle whether Earth's negotiators had bounded harm or laundered slavery into durable law. Those arguments were left to later essays, later audits, and later registries.

Founding was an event in time. What the Confederation **is** today is a separate question — one this survey does not attempt to answer.
