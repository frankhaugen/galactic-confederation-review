## Editorial note

This article is republished due to renewed cross-polity debate over guardianship law, abolition history, and the human role in shaping the accession-era compromise.

The Review notes that several cited replies to Professor Hayes have not been selected for republication.

## Abstract

Humanity entered Confederation diplomacy with abolition as part of its claimed common heritage. Yet the compromise that emerged did not eliminate legal domination. It renamed, restricted, administered, and stabilized it. This article argues that guardianship represents both a material improvement over chattel slavery and a moral stain on humanity's diplomatic legacy.
