## Abstract

Interstellar commerce depends on stable identifiers for volumes of space that may contain no habitable world, no resident polity, and no reason to visit except a filing requirement. The Confederacy Stellar Registry therefore maintains at least three distinct naming layers: the registry key, the display name, and—where applicable—the staging tag. Confusion among these layers produces predictable litigation, insurance denials, and bridge arguments that sound authoritative while referring to different legal objects. This bulletin explains the distinction in plain registrar language and offers worked examples from void-survey volumes, belt-industrial systems, and charter-marketed display names.

</div>

## Article

### 1. Why Registrars Exist

People ask why a civilization with faster-than-light transit still needs a bureau that argues about strings of letters and digits.

The answer is not nostalgia for paperwork.

The answer is that **legibility is a public good** and **places are expensive to agree on**.

A star system is not a single object in law. It is a bundle:

* a gravitational anchor or anchors
* a surveyed ephemeris record
* a volume claim filed under Confederacy convention
* optional worlds, belts, stations, lanes, and holding orbits
* insurance templates that must attach somewhere
* customs interfaces that must know which polity's rules apply at which dock

Crews want one word they can say on the bridge.

Registries want one key that still means the same thing after a refinery explodes, a habitat renames itself for tourism, or a survey buoy is upgraded into a full customs node.

Those desires conflict.

The registry's job is not to make conflict disappear. Its job is to make conflict **filing-shaped**.

### 2. Registry Key

The **registry key** is the machine-stable identifier.

Filings prefer it. Ephemeris dumps prefer it. Insurance templates prefer it. Arbitration clauses written by people who have been sued before prefer it.

Common forms include:

* bare letter-digit codes: `D7821`, `K88102`
* prefixed procedural phrases: `System D98754`
* long survey rows that signal low promotion: `D347298`
* stacked logistics qualifiers when several administrative layers must be distinguished: `System Omega Delta 83-Beta`

The registry key is allowed to be ugly.

Ugly is a feature. Ugly survives translation errors, oral transmission on noisy channels, and the tendency of tired watch officers to omit articles.

A registry key says: **this filing row refers to this surveyed volume**, not **this is what your grandmother called home**.

Void-survey and belt-industrial systems often have **no display name at all**. The code is the name because nobody with marketing budget has intervened. A star, some rocks, a beacon, and a filing cabinet in registry orbit do not constitute a polity. They constitute an address.

Students sometimes object that this is dehumanizing.

Registrars reply that dehumanizing is a strong word for a volume with no humans.

### 3. Display Name

The **display name** is what people say.

It may equal the registry key when nobody cares.

It may be a separate tradename when somebody does:

* `Gamma Trianguli IV` as cultural packaging around a registry object that also answers to a longer code
* `Pacifica Prime system` when the world brand dominates the volume
* `Taishan-7 Reach` when a settler charter wants heritage visible in port signage

Display names are not lies. They are **interface choices**.

A charter company may sell passage to a pretty name while customs receives the key. A habitat council may vote to rename a district without changing the stellar filing. A founding myth may attach to a Greek-letter constellation flavor string that was never intended to map one-to-one to pre-Confederation Earth astronomy.

The registry records display names in the Secondary Display Names Bureau because they change more often than keys and because they cause more fights.

Fights follow a pattern:

1. A crew speaks the display name.
2. A form requires the registry key.
3. Someone insists they are the same because they refer to the same sky.
4. A court asks the registry which object was insured.
5. Someone learns that **referring to the same sky** is not the same as **referring to the same filing**.

### 4. Staging Tag

The third layer confuses everyone.

A **staging tag** describes operational state on buoys, route files, and holding-orbit metadata. It answers questions like: *which lane lattice is active this cycle?* *which registry shell is accepting provisional clearances?*

Example pattern from public route literature: **7-Delta-19** as staging grammar on a system that also carries a stellar key such as **System P7D19**.

The staging tag is not a substitute for the stellar key unless you are deliberately speaking pure filing language—and even then, insurers will ask impolite questions.

Bridge crews sometimes read staging tags aloud as if they were star names because the tag is long, official, and sounds like authority.

Registrars call this **procedural majesty**: the tendency of administrative grammar to impersonate place.

If you clear for **7-Delta-19** when the form required **P7D19**, you have not gone to the wrong star. You have attached your movement to the wrong **state row** inside the right volume. That is enough for a denial.

### 5. Worked Confusions

#### Case A — The Refinery Is Not The System

**Registry key:** `D7821`

**Display name:** `D7821` (code-forward)

**Location filings:** Refinery 7, Docking Ring C, and other facility-scale objects

A hauler lists destination **D7821** on the manifest and **Refinery 7** on the oral clearance. Customs approves. Insurance denies after a casualty because the policy attached to facility subfile **Refinery 7 / Berth Stack 2**, while the captain believed the entire system was covered by naming the star.

The registry's position is unpopular and correct: **the system is not the refinery**.

Facility names belong in location fields. System keys belong in system fields. Mixing them is how people discover their hull was insured in the wrong row.

#### Case B — Pretty Name, Same Key

**Registry key:** `R902114`

**Display name:** `Epsilon Carinae II` (charter marketing)

Passenger contracts sell the display name. Arbitration clauses reference the key. A delay dispute turns on which name controlled force-majeure notice. The registry supplies both strings and a linkage table. The court rules that **passengers may reasonably rely on the display name for human purposes** while **commercial filings must use the key**.

This is why competent operators keep both on the bridge and teach new crew which form wants which.

#### Case C — The Survey That Never Got Renamed

**Registry key:** `D347298`

**Display name:** none promoted

Seven-digit tails signal late survey epoch or low promotion. No rename committee has met because no polity has budgeted a myth. The volume contains a beacon and sparse belt traffic.

A novelist visiting the registry archive once called this sad.

Registrar Orven notes that sadness is not a filing category.

### 6. Naming Families and Why Variety Is Not Chaos

Registry style guides describe weighted families of names: bureaucratic catalog strings, English placemaking, Greek-letter constellation flavor, romanized East Asian port forms, deliberate exotic transliterations.

Variety is not aesthetic indulgence.

It records **who named the place and why**:

* extractive industry leaves code-forward volumes
* charter tourism buys display names
* heritage survey teams leave syllable stacks in port records
* low-volume exotic routes keep one memorable transliteration per arc so as not to blur into noise

The registry does not judge taste. It records linkage.

When a new system is invented for fiction, manuscript, or prospectus, the useful question is not *what sounds cool on the bridge?* The useful question is: **which layer is being named, and who will sue when the layers diverge?**

### 7. Relationship to Ship Registry

Readers of vessel-law essays sometimes ask whether stellar registry is just ship registry with more hydrogen.

It is not.

Ship registry answers **who is liable under which flag fiction while moving**.

Stellar registry answers **where the filing thinks gravity and jurisdiction attach**.

The interfaces meet at customs, insurance, and route clearance. They remain separate because a hull can change registration without changing the star it serves, and a star volume can gain a refinery without becoming a ship.

Confusion between ship titles and stellar names is a cousin of confusion between display names and keys. The Review has republished enough mercantile law for one cycle. This bulletin addresses the other half of the clearance form.

### 8. Practical Guidance for Non-Registrars

If you are not a registrar, you still need three habits:

1. **Ask which layer a form wants.** System key, display name, facility location, and staging tag are different fields for different purposes.
2. **Do not treat oral bridge language as filing authority.** The bridge is for coordination. The form is for liability.
3. **Assume void systems are code-forward.** If nobody marketed the volume, the code is the place for legal purposes even if it feels inhuman on the tongue.

If you are a registrar, you need a fourth habit: **do not enjoy the litigation too openly**. Professionalism matters.

### 9. Conclusion

The Confederacy Stellar Registry is often described as the bureau that kills poetry.

Registrar Orven prefers: the bureau that keeps poetry from voiding insurance.

A registry name is a durable handle on a surveyed volume.

A display name is how people live with the volume in language.

A staging tag is what the route file says about operational state this cycle.

The place—if place means anything at interstellar scale—is the intersection of physics, filing, and the institutions that agree to treat a coordinate bundle as real for purposes of debt, salvage, and customs.

Crews will keep saying one word.

Courts will keep asking which word was filed.

The registry will keep answering.

That is the job.
