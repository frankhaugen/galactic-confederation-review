## Abstract

The Interstellar Union of Aligned Societies failed not because its members were insincere, but because it treated moral alignment as the basis of membership in a map where ethics did not sort cleanly by geometry. The Galactic Confederation that followed is best understood as institutional design after that failure: legibility and interoperability substituted for shared civilizational virtue.

</div>

## Article

### The Lesson Misread

The Compact of Five proved that shared procedure could bind a small, compatible region across species and habitats. Its success produced two lessons. One was inspiring: different peoples could live inside durable political order without immediate homogenization. The other was dangerous: if procedural integration worked locally, moral integration should work at scale.

That misread produced the Interstellar Union of Aligned Societies.

The IUAS asked whether systems could belong together ethically. The Compact had asked only whether they could transact reliably. The second question sounds more advanced. In practice it made the union less stable.

### The Moral Floor

IUAS members were not required to be culturally identical. They were expected to converge on a recognizable moral floor: personhood norms, bounded labor coercion or abolitionist trajectories, due-process language, rights-bearing legitimacy, and humanitarian obligations treated as real duties rather than local custom.

For polities raised inside the Compact mesh, this felt like the natural next step. Trade had already standardized. Arbitration had already converged. Administrative expectations had already aligned. Why should ethics lag behind procedure?

Because nearby systems were not sorted into aligned members and incompatible outsiders. The actual map produced member clusters, corridor dependencies, non-member systems between aligned neighbors, and surrounding arcs where aligned polities wrapped around incompatible ones.

A moral union in space is also a geometry problem.

### Enclosure Without Conquest

The IUAS did not need a conquest doctrine to become threatening. Its existence changed traffic toward aligned corridors, concentrated clearing inside the union, and made membership look like moral recognition while exclusion looked like civilizational judgment.

Non-member polities were not merely outside the club. They were bypassed, enclosed, and vulnerable to moral language functioning as economic pressure.

Defenders called the union non-expansionist. Surrounded neighbors experienced it as a moral empire that refused to admit what its shape was doing.

### Existential Disagreement

Inside a procedural framework, disagreement can remain narrow: contract terms, registry status, insurance eligibility, arbitration scope. Inside a moral framework, disagreement expands.

If membership requires being the right kind of polity, excluded neighbors become ethically deficient, politically suspect, and permanent reform targets. Trade, asylum, labor law, custody, policing, and intervention all become referenda on legitimacy.

The IUAS could not solve incompatible systems surrounded by aligned ones. Leaving them alone tacitly accepted moral failure inside the union's logistical environment. Pressuring them looked coercive. Intervening looked imperial. Admitting them without convergence broke the premise.

There was no stable answer.

### What Survived the Collapse

The IUAS failure taught the lesson that made the Galactic Confederation possible: interstellar order cannot rely on universal ethics as its primary organizing principle.

This is not a claim that morality is false. Moral convergence may happen locally. Moral language is politically volatile at scale. A civilization-spanning interface must survive contact with incompatible polities.

The GC kept credits, arbitration, insurance, standards, registries, and port legibility. It abandoned universal ethical membership tests and political identity built around aligned civilization.

Its premise became narrower and harsher: you do not need to be good in the same way. You need to be legible, attributable, and interoperable.

### Anti-Moralism as Design

The Confederation is therefore expressly not moralistic in the institutional sense. It does not claim one universal civilizational ethic. It does not condition participation on deep value convergence. It prefers procedural floors to ethical unity.

This is not because founders stopped caring. It is because they learned that making morality the entry condition fractures the order itself.

Critics call this abdication. Defenders call it realism. Both descriptions capture part of the design.

The GC can include polities impossible to reconcile inside the IUAS. It remains morally compromised by design because interoperability with coercive institutions is a feature of the rail, not an accident.

### Historical Verdict

The Compact succeeded because it was small and compatible.

The IUAS failed because it tried to make ethics scale faster than geometry.

The GC survived because it stopped pretending interstellar civilization needed one moral language before it could have one operating system.

That operating system is not virtuous. It is durable.
